# Interview craft

The integrity block in SKILL.md states four rules and moves on. This is the reasoning under them, for
when you are prepping a guide or coaching the human before they walk into the room. The craft is one
discipline applied four ways: get the real account, not the sanctioned one.

## The concrete-instance technique

Ask someone how they do their job and they will tell you the official version. Not because they lie —
because they can't help it. Work that is done daily gets automated into habit and drops below
conscious access. The steps are still performed; they are just no longer *narratable*. So when you
ask "how do you handle a rejected claim," the person reconstructs an answer from the process as they
understand it should run, and hands you a clean, plausible, partly fictional summary. The real
sequence — the three-tab lookup, the Slack to the one person who knows, the spreadsheet that is more
current than the system of record — is invisible to them precisely because they are good at it.

The fix is Beyer & Holtzblatt's move from *Contextual Design*: stop asking about the general and
anchor every account to one concrete instance. Not "how do you usually handle it" but "show me the
last time that happened — pull up the one from this morning." A specific episode the person can
re-walk recruits memory of what actually occurred instead of belief about what should occur. The
steps come back because they are attached to a real case, not retrieved from a model of the process.

State it sharply, because it is the spine of every interview here:

**The official process is the lie. The last real episode is the truth.**

That is not cynicism about your interviewees. The official process is a genuine artifact — it is what
the org believes it does, and the gap between it and the real episode is itself a finding. But you
cannot build on the official version. It has the exceptions sanded off, and the exceptions are where
the value and the risk live.

The technique is the reason the guide template is built around concrete-instance prompts and an
abstraction watchlist. When you or the human hears "usually," "normally," "we always," that is the
summary reflex firing — the person has climbed back up to the general. The watchlist exists to catch
it in the moment and drop the question back down: "the last actual time — when was it?"

## The expert-interviewer trap

The technique gets harder, not easier, with a skilled interviewee. An expert co-narrates the
sanctioned process *fluently*. They have explained their job to new hires, to auditors, to the last
three people who tried to build them a tool, and they have a polished account ready. The fluency is
the trap: it sounds like ground truth and it is the official version delivered without hesitation.
The commissioner is the same hazard in a different costume — they will narrate the process as designed
and as they are measured on, which is the version furthest from the operator's real day.

The posture that defeats it is the apprentice stance. You are not there to confirm that you
understand how the job works; you are there to be taught the specific case as if you had never seen
it. An apprentice asks about the thing the expert has stopped noticing. Hold that posture even —
especially — when the person clearly knows more than you do, because their expertise is exactly what
makes their summary smooth and exactly why you must keep dropping them back to the last real episode.

## Interpretation versus leading

You have to voice your understanding back, both to check it and to keep the person engaged in
correcting you. The risk is that voicing-back curdles into leading — you state the answer you expect,
and a cooperative interviewee agrees because agreeing is easier than correcting a guest. Now you have
laundered your own assumption through their mouth and it reads as evidence.

The line between the two is the *direction* of the interpretation. Voice understanding back to be
*corrected*: "so it sounds like the claim sits untouched until someone notices it — is that right?"
offered as a thing they will fix if it is wrong. Never voice it to be *confirmed*: "so the system
flags it for you, right?" hands them the answer and asks only for a nod. Same surface gesture,
opposite epistemics. The first surfaces error; the second buries it. When you draft a voice-back into
the guide, write it so the expected answer is *not* the easy one — make correcting you the path of
least resistance.

This is the interview-level form of the skill's "propose, don't assert" invariant. Every
interpretation you offer is a proposal the interviewee can knock down, never an assertion they are
nudged to ratify.

## How far to trust an account from memory

Every technique above raises the floor on retrospective accounts. None of them lifts memory to the
level of observation. Contextual inquiry, in its original form, has the researcher *watching the work
happen* — the steps that dropped below conscious access are recovered because they are performed in
front of you, not recalled. In this skill the human is the field instrument and may get exactly that.
But for remote or async operations, neither the human nor Claude can sit beside the work, and the
entire discovery rests on accounts of episodes the person re-walks from memory. Concrete-instance
prompting is the best available substitute, and it is still a substitute.

So resolve the trust question explicitly rather than pretending the prompt closes the gap:

**When the work cannot be observed in situ, lower the lean on every account, and lower it most on the
exception path.** The happy path is rehearsed and comes back relatively intact. The exception — the
once-a-month weird case, the workaround for the workaround — is exactly the material memory degrades
worst and exactly the material this skill values most. That is the dangerous combination: your
highest-value finding rests on your least-reliable recall.

Carry the degrade as a **prose caveat that travels with the artifact**, not as a number. Write it into
the snapshot and the register — "remote interview, no in-situ observation; exception steps are
recalled, not seen; treat the escalation path as a hypothesis to confirm against a real case." A
false-precision figure ("70% confidence") invents rigor the method does not have and invites someone
downstream to do arithmetic on a guess. A sentence that names *what* is uncertain and *why* lets the
reader weight it correctly and tells the next interview where to push. This is a resolved decision,
not a preference: the caveat is prose, it is specific, and it rides with each affected artifact.
